In my spring semester of junior year at Vanderbilt, all of my classes (heat transfer, mechatronics, deep learning, and microcontrollers) culminated in final projects. The instructions for deep learning and mechatronics were simple: make something cool. Since I have always been interested in developing intelligence for robotic applications, I figured this would be the perfect opportunity to combine the skill set I had learned from both classes to make a combined project. In isolation, both of these subject areas alone allow for some neat inventions. However, the intersection of the two allows for seriously powerful, intelligent, and useful creations.

When I embarked on this project, I wanted to make it something huge. It was more than a class project to me - it was a way to push my design skills to the limits and put myself in a situation in which I was constantly learning. In my years of robotics competitions, I have developed plenty of experience in designing mechatronic systems. Similarly, I have been studying and working on machine learning and robot intelligence for the majority of college. Therefore, I wouldn't learn anything from just meeting the bare requirements of each of the projects. So instead, I set my personal standards for the projects much higher than the class requirements, almost to a level I knew I would struggle to accomplish in the 1 month timeframe of the project

My final goal to my project was to prove to myself that I was, in fact, capable of desgining and implementing a large scale robotics project end-to-end. I have always been proud of my knowledge of the mechanical, electrical, and software aspects of robotics (some stronger than others). However, I have always worked on robotics projects in teams where I am not the sole contributor to these systems. Because of that, I always had the slight doubt that I don't really know as much as I believe I do. With this project, I wanted to prove to myself that I both had the knowledge and the skill to implement all the necessary systems to create an intelligent mechatronic device from end-to-end.

With all of my goals in mind, I spent a couple of days brainstorming ideas for the project that would check all the boxes. Laying in bed one night, the idea hit me: a robot that plays chess against a human. But on a physical board with pieces. And also with an artificial intelligence that can beat its opponent every time (that I train myself, of course). In classic fashion, I stayed up until the sunrise ideating, conceptualizing, and organizing the project.

Project Breakdown

With such a large project and only a month to complete it, I knew that I would have to break down the project into manageable subcomponents that all integrated together to create the full system, an important skill I learned from leading the Vanderbilt robotics team. When breaking the project down, I defined exactly what functionality each subcomponent should provide and how it interfaces with all of the other subcomponents. This structure provides multiple advantages. Firstly, with defined outputs, there are clear goals to accomplish to "complete" each subsystem, allowing me to quantify the progress on each system clearly throughout the design process. Additionally, since all of the interfaces were well defined, I could develop all of the systems in parallel and simulate the inputs to each system. If I had not done this, I would have had to build the technology stack layer by layer serially, which would have taken much longer than the month I had.
